Alzheimer's Clinical Assessment Guide

Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE)

A 30-point questionnaire testing orientation, memory, and cognitive skills. Takes 5–10 minutes.

Score interpretation

010182430
24–30Normal cognition
18–23Mild cognitive impairment
10–17Moderate dementia
0–9Severe dementia

What is tested (30 points total)

Orientation to time (5 pts)Year, season, month, date, day of week
Orientation to place (5 pts)Country, state, city, building, floor/room
Registration (3 pts)Repeat 3 unrelated words (e.g. "apple, table, penny")
Attention & calculation (5 pts)Serial 7s (subtract 7 from 100, five times) or spell "WORLD" backwards
Recall (3 pts)Recall the 3 words from Registration after a delay
Language (8 pts)Name 2 objects, repeat a phrase, follow a 3-step command, read & obey, write a sentence
Visuospatial (1 pt)Copy intersecting pentagons
Clinical Tip In resource-constrained settings, MMSE can be administered by a trained nurse or healthcare worker — no specialist neurologist is required. It takes no equipment beyond pen and paper.

Functional Assessment Staging Test (FAST)

A 7-stage scale tracking how Alzheimer's impairs daily functioning over time, from normal aging to very severe dementia.

Stage breakdown

Stage 1Normal adult — no functional decline
Stage 2Subjective deficit only — forgets names, misplaces items
Stage 3Mild — difficulty with complex work tasks (detectable by coworkers)
Stage 4Moderate — difficulty managing finances, shopping, travel
Stage 5Moderately severe — needs help choosing clothes, gets lost
Stage 6Severe — needs help dressing, bathing, toileting; incontinence
Stage 7Very severe — loss of speech, ambulation, and basic motor skills

How to assess

Ask a family caregiver or the patient directly about changes in: work performance, ability to handle finances, getting lost in familiar places, ability to dress/bathe independently, and communication skills.
Stages are assigned based on the most impaired function the patient can no longer perform reliably.
Clinical Context FAST is particularly useful because it relies on caregiver-reported observations, not equipment. It's well-suited to home visits or community clinics where caregivers accompany patients.

Memory Complaints (Subjective Cognitive Decline)

Captures the patient's own perception of memory decline, often preceding objective deficits by years.

Common screening questions

Frequency of forgettingDo you forget where you put everyday objects (keys, glasses, wallet) more than before?
Name recallDo you have difficulty remembering names of people you know well?
Conversation memoryDo you forget the content of conversations shortly after they happen?
RepetitionDo others tell you that you repeat questions or stories?
Appointment recallDo you forget appointments, anniversaries, or medications?
Getting lostDo you get confused or disoriented in familiar places?
Functional concernDo your memory difficulties affect your daily life or work?

Scoring approach

0–1 yesNo significant subjective concern
2–3 yesMild subjective complaint — monitor
4+ yesSignificant self-reported decline — follow up with objective testing
Insight Memory complaints alone are not diagnostic. However, when combined with MMSE and ADL scores, they significantly improve prediction accuracy.

Behavioral Problems (NPI)

Behavioral and psychological symptoms of dementia (BPSD) are strong diagnostic markers and drive caregiver distress.

Key behavioral domains to assess

Agitation / aggression Depression / dysphoria Anxiety Apathy / indifference Disinhibition Irritability Sleep disturbance Hallucinations Delusions Wandering

How to measure — caregiver interview

PresenceHas the patient shown this behavior in the past month? (Yes / No)
FrequencyHow often? (1-4 scale)
SeverityHow disruptive? (1-3 scale)
Domain scoreFrequency × Severity (max 12 per domain). Sum all domains for total score.

Total NPI score guidance

0–3Minimal behavioral symptoms
4–11Mild — monitor, caregiver education
12–35Moderate — consider intervention
36+Severe — specialist referral warranted

Activities of Daily Living (ADL)

ADL scales measure how independently a person can perform everyday tasks via Katz (basic) and Lawton (instrumental) scales.

Katz ADL — 6 basic self-care tasks

Scoring: 1 = independent, 0 = needs helpBathing, Dressing, Toileting, Transferring, Continence, Feeding
6/6Fully independent
4–5Mild impairment
2–3Moderate impairment
0–1Severe impairment — dependent in most activities

Lawton IADL — 8 complex daily tasks

Each scored 0 (unable) to 1 (independent)Telephone use, Shopping, Food preparation, Housekeeping, Laundry, Transportation, Managing medications, Handling finances
7–8Fully independent in community living
5–6Mild — some assistance needed
3–4Moderate — cannot live alone safely
0–2Severe — dependent care required
Key Insight IADL decline (especially managing finances, medications, and using a phone) typically precedes MMSE decline by 1–2 years.
